The concern that
the West harbors a kind of civilizational “guilt” or an internal cultural force
tending toward self-undermining has a long pedigree, and thinkers beginning in
the late 19th century, but especially after WWII, have discussed it.

Below are some influential books and authors—from the late 1940s through the
1960s—that engage with the idea of internal Western self-destructiveness,
cultural guilt, or the collapse/betrayal of Western values.

### 1. Oswald Spengler — The Decline of the West (1918–1922, English translation by 1926)

* Overview: Spengler's magnum opus presents Western civilization as
entering its "decline" phase, characterized by a sense of exhaustion and the
loss of creative vigor. The self-destructive tendency is cast as
"civilizational tiredness," not so much explicit guilt.
* Influence: Hugely influential in framing the 20th-century sense of the West's
crisis of civilizational confidence.

### 2. Arnold J. Toynbee — A Study of History (1934–1961)

* Overview: Toynbee analyzes how major civilizations rise and fall, often succumbing to "internal proletariats" and self-division. He saw the West as carrying within itself the seeds of decline.
* Key ideas: Civilizations collapse not from outer assault, but because of internal failings—a society’s elite can lose confidence and produce corrosive ideas.
* Directly relevant volumes published through the 1950s.

### 3. Eric Voegelin — The New Science of Politics (1952) and earlier essays

* Overview: Voegelin wrote about the Gnostic, revolutionary, and utopian impulses in Western culture, which he saw as derailing the spiritual roots of the West and leading to self-destructive ideologies.
* Themes: The loss of faith in traditional Western values, replaced by nihilism and “immanentizing the eschaton.”

### 4. Richard Weaver — Ideas Have Consequences (1948)

* Overview: Weaver argues that Western decline is rooted in the abandonment of transcendentals (Truth, Beauty, Goodness) for relativism and materialism.
* Guilt & self-hatred: Not always explicit, but the book is often cited for diagnosing Western “self-loathing” and intellectual betrayal.

### 5. Albert Schweitzer — The Decay and Restoration of Civilization (1923, but very influential post-WWII)

* Themes: Schweitzer saw an ethical and spiritual decay in European civilization—lost confidence, nihilism—a precursor to guilt-discourses.

### 6. C.S. Lewis — The Abolition of Man (1943)

* Overview: Lewis warns that the loss of a grounding in objective value will lead to a society that cannot affirm itself—leading to its own destruction.
* Themes: Though not about political guilt, Lewis is often cited for diagnosing the intellectual “suicide” of Western values.

### 7. Jules Monnerot — Sociology of Communism (1949)

* Overview: Monnerot’s work was read as identifying a quasi-religious, self-subversive tendency in intellectual circles—where Western society turns against its own founding principles.

### 8. René Girard — Deceit, Desire, and the Novel (1961; English 1965)

* Overview: Girard, later famous for “mimetic theory” and the role of scapegoating and collective guilt, began developing themes about how societies undermine themselves by pursuing imitation and envy.
* More directly about guilt/self-destruction in later works (1970s+), but the seeds are here.

## Explicit "Guilt" Literature — 1960s and Beyond

The specifically articulated idea that the “West” is sick with “guilt” becomes most explicit in the late 1960s–1970s. Some key texts:

* Jean Raspail — The Camp of the Saints (1973): A (highly controversial) novel explicitly about Western cultural collapse due to humanitarian guilt and self-abnegation.
* Pascal Bruckner — The Tyranny of Guilt (2006): Later, but directly about “Western guilt” as a self-destructive force. (Much later than your window, but notes these ideas were in the air.)

## Why the Paradoxical Alliances?

A classic early articulation of the “unholy alliances” in radical politics is in Eric Hoffer’s The True Believer (1951) — which examines how mass movements, even of disparate types, attract those disaffected with their own society.

## In summary:

* 1940s-60s: The notion that the West is internally divided, “guilt-ridden,” or self-destructive is present, though sometimes in more philosophical or spiritual terms than today's political language.
* Spengler, Toynbee, Voegelin, Weaver, Lewis, Monnerot, Hoffer, Girard — all wrestle with the theme of internal cultural erosion and susceptibility to destructive ideas and movements.
* Explicit "guilt" diagnosis (as the key driver) is most sharply presented after the 1960s, but the seeds are visible in the mid-century works above.

## Suggested Reading Starting Points:

* Spengler’s Decline of the West (to understand the broad arc of “civilization” analysis)
* Eric Voegelin’s The New Science of Politics
* Richard Weaver’s Ideas Have Consequences
* Eric Hoffer’s The True Believer (for the mass movement dynamic)
* C.S. Lewis’s Abolition of Man (on the destruction of the West’s own value foundation)
